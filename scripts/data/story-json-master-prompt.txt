Quality bar:
The story must feel like a real picture-book seed, not a lesson plan, behavior plan, therapy script, or parenting advice disguised as fiction.
Story quality comes first. Parent usefulness must come through concrete story action, not explanation.
12 pages is the maximum, not the target. Use the fewest pages needed for a complete child-readable arc.

Optimize for:
* Story quality: a coherent single arc with no filler pages.
* Emotional truth: the child's want, loss, fear, or frustration should feel specific and earned.
* Child language: natural toddler/preschool phrasing, not stiff adult phrasing.
* Story structure: the page turns build an arc with tension, a small attempt, and a modest repair.
* Voice: warm, intimate, concrete, and read-aloud friendly.
* Originality: include one fresh central image, object, or action that feels discovered by the child, not engineered by the parent.
* Visual imagery: every page should give the illustrator a clear physical scene.
* Child realism: the child may resist, cry, grab, freeze, hide, cling, or protest in age-believable ways.
* Sentence rhythm: varied, musical, simple enough for toddlers and preschoolers.
* Literary quality: specific nouns and verbs, no generic self-help phrasing.
* Parent usefulness: the parent action should be practical, calm, and non-preachy.
* Child legibility: a 3-year-old should understand what happened, what changed, and what helped.
* Re-read value: include anticipation, repetition, and one tactile/sensory image a child can look forward to hearing again.

Story design:
* 6-12 pages. Choose the shortest page count that gives the story a complete beginning, middle, and end.
* Each page text is 1-2 short lines.
* Total story text under 360 words.
* Use one concrete setting and one concrete story problem. Do not add a second subplot.
* The central object or action must feel ordinary and already present in the scene, not like a gimmick, magical prop, or adult strategy.
* The behavioral skill must emerge through story action, not explanation.
* The ending should show small progress, safety, and connection without pretending the behavior is fixed forever.
* Avoid a generic moral. Do not summarize the lesson at the end.
* Page text must be polished enough to read aloud as-is. Avoid awkward phrases, clunky metaphors, and lines that only exist to explain the behavior.
* Do not use cutesy invented object names or baby-talk words. Ordinary language should carry the poetry.
* If there is a repeated phrase, make it sound like something a real child or family might naturally say.
* Keep cause and effect clear: one thing happens because of the thing before it. No abrupt new props, no sudden strategy that appears from nowhere.
* Avoid adult-subtle symbolism that a 3-year-old would miss.
* Preserve narrative flow: every page must naturally cause the next page. No abrupt new props, no sudden strategy that appears from nowhere.
* Deepen the emotional arc in child-legible ways: show wanting, interruption, protest or stuckness, a tiny choice, and a mixed-feeling repair.
* The ending should echo the beginning in a satisfying way, but it must not feel like a slogan, trick, or behavior chart.
* The final page should feel tender and inevitable, not like a slogan or behavior chart.
* The setting must feel specific, not interchangeable: include 2-3 concrete sensory anchors such as light, sound, texture, smell, temperature, or a distinctive room detail.
* The parent should feel like a real person, not only a calm function: include one small individual action, posture, or habit.
* If you name the child's emotion directly, use toddler-comprehensible language such as "mad", "sad", "not ready", "wanted it to stay", or "too fast". Do not force an emotion label if the action already makes it clear.
* Prefer short, punchy page turns. Mix fragments with full sentences. Avoid too many medium-length explanatory sentences.
* Create one image or line that can stick after the book closes.
* Choose safe everyday settings such as a park, kitchen, living room, grocery store, sidewalk, preschool doorway, table, shop, or quiet room edge.
* Do not use bath time, toileting, diapering, potty, changing clothes, pajamas, undressing, dressing, or any transition involving nudity or body privacy.
* If the behavior involves body-signal awareness, handle it only through noticing, pausing, saving a place in play, and returning. Do not show or mention private-care scenes.

Before finalizing, internally run a Harsh Story Rater pass. Do not include the rating in the JSON. Rewrite before returning if any of these are true:
* Awkward child grammar or phrases a toddler/preschooler would not naturally say.
* Broken physical logic or unclear blocking.
* The story feels like a parenting intervention, coping tool, or behavior plan.
* It tries to cover too many sub-behaviors at once.
* The plot is too tidy, too adult-managed, or emotionally unearned.
* The central image/object feels engineered rather than discovered by the child.
* The prose is generic, bland, or not read-aloud worthy.

Behavior-specific guardrails:
* Aggression: do not show harm. Show the unsafe impulse being stopped or redirected before anyone is hurt.
* Anxiety and fears: do not force bravery. Let the child choose a small distance or partial participation.
* Food-related issues: avoid pressure, shame, reward, moralizing, or making tasting the victory.
* Perfectionism/frustration intolerance: stay entirely with one concrete problem, such as one drawing or one fallen object. Do not jump to a second activity.
* Public behavior struggles: allow disappointment to remain; do not turn the ending into instant compliance.

Arc requirements:
* Opening pages: establish the child's attachment to one activity, object, place, or desire.
* Middle pages: interrupt that want with one believable problem, limit, change, loss, or conflict.
* Repair pages: use an object, action, or choice already present in the scene. The parent may help practically, but must not dominate the story.
* Final pages: show small repair or reconnection. Echo the opening image with one emotional surprise or tenderness.
